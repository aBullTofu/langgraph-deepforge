🏭 水渠与磨坊
🏭 The Aqueduct and the Mills

L01 · State / Node / Edge / Conditional Edge

古时有一个王国，国王下令沿河修建一条水渠，让河水依次流过五座磨坊。每座磨坊从水渠中取水，完成自己的活计——第一座磨面，第二座织布，第三座榨油，第四座打铁，第五座造纸——然后将水排回水渠，流向下游。

起初一切顺利。直到某天，第四座磨坊的坊主发现上游来的水里混着油渣，他的铁器淬火时出现了裂纹。他派人去查，才发现是第三座榨油坊换了新工艺，排水变脏了。

国王召开磨坊议会。众人争执不下，没人愿意承认是自己的问题。最后国王颁布了一条法令：每座磨坊必须在排水口挂一块木牌，写明自己往水里加了什么、去掉了什么。从那以后，下游的磨坊只要看一眼上游的木牌，就知道水流里有了什么变化。

多年后，国王的孙子继位。他发现水渠有时需要分流——比如大旱之年，灌溉田地的磨坊需要更多水，造纸的磨坊可以暂时停转。他命人在水渠上修了一座分水闸，闸口守卫根据木牌上的信息，决定水流去向："如果水里铁屑过多，说明打铁坊正在全力运转，把水全给铁坊；如果水面漂着纸浆，说明造纸坊需要水，开左闸。"

这个王国繁荣了一千年。后世的历史学家发现，他们发明的木牌流水制和分水闸，正是现代图编程中 State 和 Conditional Edge 的雏形。

Long ago, in an ancient kingdom, the king ordered an aqueduct built along the river, channeling water to flow through five mills in sequence. Each mill drew water from the aqueduct, performed its task — the first ground flour, the second wove cloth, the third pressed oil, the fourth forged iron, the fifth made paper — then returned the water to the aqueduct to flow downstream.

At first, everything ran smoothly. Until one day, the master of the fourth mill found oil residue in the upstream water, and his iron blades cracked during quenching. He sent someone to investigate and discovered the third mill — the oil press — had switched to a new process, dirtying its discharge.

The king convened the Mill Council. Everyone argued, no one admitted fault. Finally, the king issued a decree: Every mill must hang a wooden plaque at its outflow, listing what it added to the water and what it removed. From that day on, any downstream mill could glance at the upstream plaque and know exactly what had changed in the water.

Years later, the king's grandson ascended the throne. He realized the aqueduct sometimes needed to divert — during drought years, the irrigation mill needed more water, while the paper mill could temporarily halt. He ordered a diversion sluice built on the aqueduct. The gatekeeper read the wooden plaques and decided the water's path: "If the water carries too much iron dust, the forge is running at full capacity — send all water to the forge. If paper pulp floats on the surface, the paper mill needs water — open the left gate."

The kingdom prospered for a thousand years. Later historians discovered that their inventions — the Plaque-Based Flow System and the Diversion Sluice — were the earliest precursors to State and Conditional Edges in modern graph programming.

🏚 十字路口的客栈
🏚 The Inn at the Crossroads

L02 · LLM Node + Conditional Edge

丝绸之路上的商队来到一个三岔路口。路口立着一间客栈，客栈里住着一位老旅人。这位老旅人有个神奇的本事：他只要看一眼你的货物和脸色，就能准确告诉你该走哪条路。

"去长安卖丝绸？走左边，官道平坦，税关友好。"

"去波斯卖茶叶？走中间，虽然山路崎岖但路程最短，茶叶不易受潮。"

"去西域卖瓷器？走右边，经敦煌出关，有骆驼商队可以结伴。"

商人问老旅人："你怎么知道该走哪条路？"老旅人笑而不语。直到他临终前才告诉徒弟："我在这里住了四十年，看过十万支商队。他们去了哪条路、后来怎么样，我都记在心里。所以看一眼就明白。"

徒弟继承了客栈。但徒弟没有四十年的经验。于是他想了个办法：给全世界的商人写信——"如果你在这个路口，去长安应该走哪条？去波斯呢？去罗马呢？"——收集每个人的答案，综合起来。虽然没有师父那么准，但答对的概率已经相当高了。

A merchant caravan on the Silk Road arrived at a three-way fork. At the junction stood an inn, and in the inn lived an old traveler. This traveler had a remarkable gift: with a single glance at your goods and your expression, he could tell you exactly which road to take.

"Selling silk to Chang'an? Take the left — flat official road, friendly customs."

"Selling tea to Persia? Take the middle — mountain paths are rough but shortest route, tea won't get damp."

"Selling porcelain to the Western Regions? Take the right — exit through Dunhuang, you can join a camel caravan."

A merchant asked the old traveler: "How do you know which road to take?" The old traveler smiled and said nothing. Only on his deathbed did he tell his apprentice: "I've lived here forty years and watched a hundred thousand caravans. Which road they took, what happened to them later — I remember it all. That's why one glance is enough."

The apprentice inherited the inn. But the apprentice didn't have forty years of experience. So he came up with an idea: he wrote letters to merchants all over the world — "If you were at this fork, which road would you take to Chang'an? To Persia? To Rome?" — collecting everyone's answers and synthesizing them. He wasn't as precise as his master, but his probability of being right was remarkably high.

🧰 学徒的工具箱
🧰 The Apprentice's Toolbox

L03 · Tools / Tool Calling

老锁匠收了个徒弟。第一天，他给了徒弟一个上锁的铁盒："打开它。"

徒弟用手掰、用牙咬、用石头砸，折腾了一整天，铁盒纹丝不动。

第二天，老锁匠拿出一串钥匙，一把一把教徒弟："这把开挂锁，这把开暗锁，这把是万能钥匙但有些锁打不开会卡住。"然后他把钥匙挂成一串，递给徒弟："再试。"

徒弟接过钥匙串，对着铁盒试了第二把，"咔嗒"，开了。从此，遇到任何锁，徒弟不再用蛮力——翻看钥匙串，选一把最像的，插进去拧。打不开？换一把。

后来徒弟成了名匠。他的弟子问他秘诀，他说："一个锁匠的水平，不取决于他的手有多巧，而取决于他的钥匙串上挂了多少把钥匙，以及他是否知道每把钥匙能开什么锁。"

An old locksmith took on an apprentice. On the first day, he handed the apprentice a locked iron box: "Open it."

The apprentice pried with his hands, bit with his teeth, bashed with a rock — struggled all day. The iron box didn't budge.

The second day, the old locksmith took out a ring of keys and taught the apprentice one by one: "This one opens padlocks, this one opens concealed locks, this is a master key but it jams on some locks." Then he strung them on a keyring and handed it over: "Try again."

The apprentice took the keyring, tried the second key on the iron box — click — it opened. From then on, faced with any lock, the apprentice never used brute force again — flip through the keyring, pick the one that looks most likely, insert and turn. Doesn't open? Try another.

Later, the apprentice became a master craftsman. When his own students asked for his secret, he said: "A locksmith's skill is not measured by the dexterity of his hands, but by how many keys hang on his ring, and whether he knows which key opens which lock."

🔍 侦探的方法
🔍 The Detective's Method

L04 · ReAct Loop

福尔摩斯不是神。他有一个方法。

每接到一个案子，他不会直接给出答案。他会先推理："这个脚印说明凶手身高六尺；窗台上的烟灰是印度雪茄，说明凶手有海外关系；壁炉里的未烧完的纸片是一张船票——我需要确认是哪条航线。"

然后他行动：派人去码头查乘客名单。

然后他观察：拿到了名单，发现一个名字和烟灰的来源对上了。

然后他继续推理："这个人的住所应该在码头附近——我再去查附近的租房记录。"

就这样，推理 → 行动 → 观察 → 推理 → 行动 → 观察，一直循环，直到他站起来说："华生，我知道凶手是谁了。"

华生问他为什么不像苏格兰场那样凭直觉办案。福尔摩斯说："直觉只能解决你已经见过一百遍的案子。对于真正的谜题，你需要一套方法。"

Holmes was no oracle. He had a method.

For every case he took on, he never gave an answer right away. First, he would reason: "These footprints suggest the killer is six feet tall. The cigar ash on the windowsill is Indian — indicating overseas connections. The half-burned paper in the fireplace is a steamship ticket — I need to identify which shipping line."

Then he would act: send someone to the docks to check the passenger manifest.

Then he would observe: the manifest came back, and one name matched the source of the cigar ash.

Then he would reason further: "This person likely lives near the docks — I'll check the nearby rental records."

And so it went: Reason → Act → Observe → Reason → Act → Observe, looping until he stood up and said: "Watson, I know who the killer is."

Watson asked him why he didn't rely on intuition like Scotland Yard. Holmes replied: "Intuition only solves cases you've already seen a hundred times. For a genuine puzzle, you need a method."

📜 书记官的账本
📜 The Scribe's Ledger

L05 · Checkpoint Persistence

古罗马的元老院有一个规矩：每一场辩论，不管持续多久，书记官必须在每个人发言结束后——不是辩论结束后——立即把刚才的发言一字不差地记在羊皮纸上。如果辩论被暴风雨或敌人的号角打断，下次开会时，书记官翻开羊皮纸，从上次中断的地方继续记录。

有一年，罗马遭受瘟疫。元老院讨论抗疫措施，辩论持续了整整三天。第三天傍晚，一场地震摧毁了元老院的穹顶。辩论中断。

一个月后，元老们在临时帐篷里重开辩论。书记官打开羊皮纸——最后一条记录是："保民官提议封城三十天，执政官尚未回应。"于是辩论从这个断点继续，仿佛地震从未发生。

千年之后，一个叫 LangGraph 的程序继承了书记官的智慧：每一个 super-step 都是一次"发言结束"，都是一条新记录。

The Roman Senate had a rule: in every debate, no matter how long it lasted, the scribe must record each speaker's words verbatim on parchment immediately after each person finished speaking — not after the debate concluded. If the debate was interrupted by a storm or enemy trumpets, at the next session the scribe would open the parchment and continue recording from where they left off.

One year, Rome was struck by plague. The Senate debated pandemic countermeasures for three full days. On the evening of the third day, an earthquake destroyed the Senate dome. The debate was interrupted.

A month later, the senators reconvened in makeshift tents. The scribe opened the parchment — the last entry read: "The Tribune proposed a thirty-day city lockdown. The Consul has not yet responded." The debate resumed from that exact point, as if the earthquake had never happened.

A thousand years later, a program called LangGraph inherited the scribe's wisdom: every super-step is an "end of speech," a new record.

🌉 桥上的收费站
🌉 The Toll Booth on the Bridge

L06 · Human-in-the-Loop

有一座桥，连接两个王国。桥中间设了一个收费站。不是每辆车都要停——空车直接过，普通货车自动扣费，但运输火药、运送囚犯、装载金银的车，必须在收费站停下，由守桥官亲自检查货物清单，签字盖章后才能放行。

有人问守桥官："为什么不让所有车都自动通过？"

守桥官说："三个月前，一辆声称'运输粮食'的货车过桥时突然爆炸。如果那辆车通过的不是一座有收费站的桥，而是无人看守的通道——对面就是王宫。"

"那为什么不让所有车都停下来检查？"

"那样桥就堵死了。每天只有三辆车装的可能是炸药。为了三辆车让三千辆车排队，不值得。"

There was a bridge connecting two kingdoms, with a toll booth in the middle. Not every vehicle had to stop — empty carts passed freely, ordinary freight wagons were auto-charged, but vehicles carrying gunpowder, transporting prisoners, or loaded with gold and silver had to halt at the booth. The bridge officer personally inspected the cargo manifest, signed and stamped, before granting passage.

Someone asked the bridge officer: "Why not let all vehicles pass automatically?"

The officer replied: "Three months ago, a wagon claiming to carry 'grain' exploded mid-bridge. If that wagon had crossed a bridge without a toll booth — an unguarded passage — the other side is the royal palace."

"Then why not stop and inspect every vehicle?"

"That would gridlock the bridge. Only three wagons a day might carry explosives. Making three thousand wagons queue for the sake of three — it's not worth it."

📨 信使与碎片
📨 The Messenger and the Fragments

L07 · Streaming

两个国王通信。起初，国王 A 让书记官写完一整封信，封好，交给信使，骑马三天送到国王 B 手中。

国王 B 经常急得跳脚——第一句话可能就回答了核心问题，但他要等三天才能看到。

后来，一个聪明的书记官发明了一种新的通信方式：他写好一句话，就把这张纸片交给等在一旁的快马信使；写第二句，第二个信使出发；写第三句，第三个信使出发……

此后，国王 B 在第一天就能收到开头几句，先知道大意；第二天收到中间几段；第三天收到结尾和签名。虽然总时间没有变短，但国王 B 的感受完全不同——"我不再是在黑夜里等待一封不知何时到达的信了。"

Two kings exchanged letters. At first, King A had his scribe write an entire letter, seal it, hand it to a messenger, who rode three days to deliver it to King B.

King B often paced with frustration — the first sentence might have already answered the core question, but he had to wait three days to read it.

Then, a clever scribe invented a new method: after writing each sentence, he handed that slip of paper to a waiting express courier. After the second sentence, a second courier departed. After the third, a third courier set off...

From then on, King B received the opening lines on the first day and grasped the gist; the middle passages on the second day; the conclusion and signature on the third. Though the total time hadn't shortened, King B's experience was utterly transformed — "I am no longer waiting in the dark for a letter that might arrive at any unknown moment."

🏰 城墙上的四道门
🏰 The Four Gates on the City Wall

L08 · Fault Tolerance & Error Handling

一座要塞修了四道城墙。外敌侵犯时：

第一道门设在最外层——弓箭手朝敌人射三箭，如果敌人仍然冲过来，门自动关闭。这是给"小股骚扰"准备的，大部分敌人在第一道门就被挡住了。

第二道门是陷阱——敌人冲进来后，地面塌陷。但守军会喊话："投降，给你吊上来。"敌人如果愿意投降，还能活。

第三道门是一间密室——守军把信鸽放出去，向友军求救："我们遇到麻烦了，你们有什么建议？"信鸽带回来的指令可能是"再坚持一天"或"撤退"。这道门不是自动的，需要人的决定。

第四道门是最后一道。守军只在墙上刻一句话："下次修墙的时候，记住今天为什么被攻破。"然后将此事记录在案，撤退。

要塞从未被攻陷。不是因为城墙坚不可摧，而是因为这四道门的设计——大部分问题在前两道门就解决了，能冲到第四道门的敌人，一定是有值得学习的新攻击方式。

A fortress was built with four concentric walls. When enemies attacked:

The First Gate stood at the outermost wall — archers fired three volleys at the enemy. If they still charged, the gate automatically closed. This handled "minor harassment"; most enemies were stopped at the first gate.

The Second Gate was a trap — when enemies burst through, the ground collapsed beneath them. But the defenders would call out: "Surrender, and we'll haul you up." If the enemy chose to surrender, they could still survive.

The Third Gate was a sealed chamber — the defenders released a carrier pigeon to allied forces, asking: "We're in trouble — do you have any advice?" The pigeon might return with "hold on one more day" or "retreat." This gate was not automatic — it required a human decision.

The Fourth Gate was the last. The defenders carved only one line into the wall: "Next time you build walls, remember why they were breached today." Then they recorded the incident and withdrew.

The fortress was never conquered. Not because the walls were impenetrable, but because of the four-gate design — most problems were resolved at the first two gates. Any enemy that reached the fourth gate represented a new attack vector worth studying.

🏙 城中之城
🏙 The City Within a City

L09 · Subgraph

长安城太大，一个京兆尹管不过来。于是天子设"里坊制"：全城分为一百零八坊，每坊有自己的坊正。坊内的事情——治安、市场、清洁——坊正自己处理。坊和坊之间，通过城门和街道连接。京兆尹只管理"跨坊"的事务：主干道的秩序、城门开关、税收统算。

最巧妙的是：每个坊的内部管理方式可以完全不同。东市的管理规则是"日出开市、日落闭市"，西市是"按货物种类分区经营"，胡人聚居的坊有自己的头人和规矩。京兆尹不需要知道东市几点开门——他只需要知道"给东市的税收指标是多少，实际交了多少"。

Chang'an was too vast for a single governor to manage. So the Emperor established the "ward system": the city was divided into 108 wards, each with its own ward master. Internal ward affairs — security, markets, sanitation — were handled by the ward master. Wards connected to each other through gates and streets. The Governor only managed "cross-ward" matters: main road order, gate schedules, and tax aggregation.

The most ingenious part: each ward could operate under completely different internal rules. The East Market followed "open at sunrise, close at sunset." The West Market organized stalls by goods category. The foreign merchant quarter had its own headman and customs. The Governor didn't need to know when the East Market opened — he only needed to know "what was the East Market's tax quota, and how much did they actually pay."

🐝 蜂巢的议会
🐝 The Hive Council

L10 · Multi-Agent Collaboration

蜂巢里住着三种蜜蜂。采集蜂负责找花、采蜜；建筑蜂负责修蜂巢、调节温度；守卫蜂负责警戒外敌。起初，蜂后给每只蜂直接下达指令——但蜂巢太大了，蜂后的指令传到最远的蜂巢角落要半小时。

于是蜂巢进化出一个新制度：蜂后只做两件事——听取报告、分配大方向。她说："最近蜜源少了，采集蜂增加一倍，建筑蜂减半。"然后就不再说话了。采集蜂的队长自己去分配今天去哪片花田，建筑蜂的队长决定今天修哪面墙。

遇到特殊情况——比如一只采集蜂发现了一片从未见过的巨大花田——她会直接飞到建筑蜂的队长那里说："给我派一百只蜂，那片花田需要人手。"不需要经过蜂后。

Three kinds of bees lived in the hive. Forager bees found flowers and collected nectar. Builder bees maintained the hive and regulated temperature. Guard bees watched for outside threats. At first, the Queen gave direct orders to every bee — but the hive grew too large, and the Queen's commands took half an hour to reach the farthest corners.

So the hive evolved a new system: the Queen only did two things — listen to reports and allocate overall direction. She would say: "Nectar sources are dwindling lately — double the foragers, halve the builders." Then she fell silent. The forager captain assigned which flower fields to visit today. The builder captain decided which wall to repair.

When something unusual happened — say, a forager discovered a massive, never-before-seen flower field — she would fly directly to the builder captain and say: "Send me a hundred bees, that field needs hands." No need to go through the Queen.

🦅 将军的侦察兵
🦅 The General's Scouts

L11 · Send API + Parallel Execution

将军要在茫茫群山中找到敌军主力。他派出五名侦察兵，每人负责一个方向——东、南、西、北、中——同时出发。他自己坐在中军帐里，桌上铺着一张空白地图。

侦察兵们每隔一炷香放一只信鸽回来。将军把信鸽带来的情报标注在地图上：东面是悬崖，无人；北面有马蹄印，约三百骑兵；西面有炊烟，可能是营地……

两名侦察兵一去不回。但将军没有等他们——他根据已收到的三份情报做出了判断：敌军在北面。他率军向北，打了胜仗。战后第三天，失踪的侦察兵才回来——原来遇到了山洪，绕了一大圈。

一位文官问将军："为什么不派一个侦察兵把所有方向都看完再回来？"将军答："一个人看完五个方向要五天。五个人同时看各自的方向只要一天。而且就算有两人生病了，剩下三个人的情报也够我用。"

A general needed to locate the enemy's main force somewhere in a vast mountain range. He dispatched five scouts, each assigned to one direction — east, south, west, north, center — all departing simultaneously. He sat in his command tent, a blank map spread across the table.

At regular intervals, each scout released a carrier pigeon with intelligence. The general marked every report on his map: east — cliffs, no enemy; north — hoofprints, roughly three hundred cavalry; west — smoke rising, likely a camp...

Two scouts never returned. But the general did not wait for them — he made his judgment based on the three reports already received: the enemy was to the north. He marched north and won the battle. Three days after the victory, the missing scouts finally returned — they had encountered a flash flood and taken a long detour.

An official asked the general: "Why not send one scout to survey all five directions before returning?" The general replied: "One man surveying five directions takes five days. Five men each surveying one direction takes one day. And even if two fall ill, the other three give me enough intelligence to act."

⚒ 从打铁铺到兵工厂
⚒ From Blacksmith Shop to Arsenal

L12 · Production Deployment

从前有一个铁匠，手艺精湛。他在村里开了间打铁铺，自己拉风箱、烧炉子、打铁、淬火、交货。全村的人都来找他打农具。

王国要发动战争，要求铁匠一个月内打造一万把剑。铁匠算了算——自己一天最多打五把，一万把要两千天。他需要一百个铁匠。

于是他做了几件事：

第一，他把自己打剑的过程写成一本手册。不是"怎么打一把好剑"的笼统描述，而是每一步——炉温多少度、铁烧多久、锤几下、淬火用什么油、冷却多久——精确到新来的学徒也能看懂。

第二，他建了一条流水线——一个铁匠只负责烧炉，一个只负责锤打，一个只负责淬火……

第三，他在每个工位后面放了一个本子——每做完一把剑就记一笔。如果某个工位出了问题，不用从头再来，翻开本子就知道问题出在哪个环节。

第四，他在城里设了一个展示间，官员们可以随时来看：现在打造了多少把剑、每把剑的质量如何、哪个工位最快。

一个月后，一万把剑如期交付。铁匠被封为兵部尚书。

Once there was a blacksmith of extraordinary skill. He ran a small shop in his village — working the bellows himself, firing the furnace, hammering the iron, quenching the blade, delivering the finished goods. The entire village came to him for farming tools.

Then the kingdom went to war and demanded the blacksmith forge ten thousand swords in a single month. The blacksmith did the math — at five swords a day max, ten thousand would take two thousand days. He needed a hundred blacksmiths.

So he did several things:

First, he wrote a manual of his sword-making process. Not a vague description of "how to forge a good sword," but every single step — furnace temperature, how long to heat the iron, how many hammer strikes, which oil for quenching, how long to cool — precise enough that even a new apprentice could follow.

Second, he built an assembly line — one smith only fired the furnace, one only hammered, one only quenched...

Third, he placed a ledger at every workstation — recording each completed sword. If a station had a problem, there was no need to start over; open the ledger and you'd know exactly which step went wrong.

Fourth, he set up a showroom in the city where officials could visit anytime: how many swords had been forged, the quality of each, which station was fastest.

One month later, ten thousand swords were delivered on schedule. The blacksmith was appointed Minister of Arms.
